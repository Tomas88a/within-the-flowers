







Bloom as witness, petal as gaze. It sees the quiet sorrow of a girl who lingers, placing a hand against its petals, her tears falling like rain, each drop a whispered secret. It feels the warmth of a child’s laughter, light as dandelion fluff, as tiny fingers brush its edges. It watches the seasons turn—spring’s soft breath, summer’s fiery embrace, autumn’s golden farewell, winter’s hush—and holds each moment like a treasure, etched into the veins of its petals.
The flower does not speak, yet it understands. It understands the fleetingness of light, the weight of silence, the beauty of moments that fade like smoke. It gazes upon the world not with longing, but with acceptance—for it knows that to bloom is to witness, to gaze is to hold, and to exist within the flowers is to be part of something eternal.

When the first light of dawn paints the sky in hues of amber and rose, the peony closes its petals, tucking the night’s stories into its heart. But its gaze lingers, a silent promise: tomorrow, it will open again, to watch, to witness, to hold the world once more—from within the flowers.
